If you live in Japan as a foreign resident, you have probably heard about "My Number" at some point. Maybe your employer asked for it. Maybe you saw a poster at city hall. But many foreigners confuse two different things: the My Number itself and the My Number Card. They are not the same, and understanding the difference matters.

This guide explains everything you need to know about the My Number Card as a foreigner living in Japan in 2026, including why you should get one, how to apply, and what documents you need.

---

## My Number vs. My Number Card: What Is the Difference?

Your "My Number" (also called Individual Number) is a 12-digit number assigned to every resident of Japan, including foreign nationals registered in the Basic Resident Register. You received this number automatically when you registered your address at your local city or ward office. It was mailed to you on a paper notification card (tsuuchi card).

The "My Number Card" (Individual Number Card / Kojin Bangou Card) is a physical IC card with your photo, name, address, date of birth, and My Number printed on it. It also contains a digital certificate that lets you use online government services.

Here is the key distinction:

- My Number = just a number (everyone has one)
- My Number Card = a physical ID card with IC chip (you must apply for it)

The paper notification card that was mailed to you is NOT a My Number Card. Many foreigners keep the paper notification and think they are done. They are not. The actual card requires a separate application.

## Why Should You Get a My Number Card?

Getting a My Number Card is not legally mandatory, but it is practically becoming essential for daily life in Japan. Here are the concrete benefits:

**1. Official Photo ID**
The My Number Card is accepted as government-issued photo identification throughout Japan. This is useful when you need ID but do not want to carry your residence card everywhere.

**2. Health Insurance Card Replacement**
As of December 2024, Japan transitioned away from traditional paper/plastic health insurance cards. The My Number Card now functions as your health insurance card at hospitals and pharmacies. If you do not have one, you may need to use alternative certificates, which adds hassle.

**3. Online Government Services (Mynaportal)**
With your My Number Card, you can access Mynaportal (myna.go.jp), the government's online portal. Through Mynaportal you can:
- Check your pension records
- View your tax information
- Complete certain administrative procedures online
- Link your health insurance information

**4. Tax Filing (e-Tax)**
If you file taxes in Japan, you can use your My Number Card to file electronically through e-Tax. No more waiting in line at the tax office during filing season.

**5. Convenience Store Services**
You can use your My Number Card at convenience store multi-copy machines to print official documents like your juminhyo (resident certificate) and tax certificates, even outside city hall business hours.

**6. Faster Procedures at City Hall**
Many municipal offices have dedicated terminals where My Number Card holders can complete procedures faster.

**7. Future Digital Services**
The Japanese government continues expanding My Number Card functionality. Having the card now means you will be ready for new services as they launch.

## How to Apply: Step-by-Step

There are several ways to apply for a My Number Card. The most common method for foreigners is the online application or applying at your local city/ward office.

### Method 1: Online Application (Recommended)

**Step 1: Prepare Your Notification Card**
Find the paper notification (tsuuchi card) you received when you registered your address. It has a QR code and your application ID on it.

If you lost your notification card, go to your local city or ward office and ask for a new application ID (kojin bangou card koufu shinseisho).

**Step 2: Take a Photo**
Take a photo that meets these requirements:
- Taken within 6 months
- Facing forward, no hats or sunglasses
- White or light plain background
- Clear focus, no shadows on face
- Size: 3.5 cm x 4.5 cm (for mail) or 480 x 640 pixels minimum (for online)

Tips: Use a white wall at home. Smartphone cameras work fine for the online application. Make sure the lighting is even and there are no shadows.

**Step 3: Submit Online**
1. Scan the QR code on your notification card
2. It opens the application website
3. Enter your email address
4. You will receive an email with a link
5. Click the link and upload your photo
6. Confirm your information and submit

The entire online process takes about 10 minutes.

**Step 4: Wait for Your Card**
Processing usually takes 3 to 8 weeks, though times vary by municipality. You will receive a postcard (hagaki) in the mail telling you your card is ready for pickup.

**Step 5: Pick Up Your Card at City Hall**
Go to your local city or ward office with:
- The pickup notification postcard
- Your residence card (zairyu card)
- Your paper notification card (if you still have it)

At the counter, you will set up two passwords:
- A 4-digit numeric PIN (used for basic services)
- A 6-to-16 character alphanumeric password (used for digital certificates like e-Tax)

Write these down and keep them safe. If you forget them, you must go back to city hall to reset them.

### Method 2: Apply at City Hall

If you prefer doing things in person:
1. Go to your local city or ward office
2. Ask for the My Number Card application form
3. Bring a photo (3.5 x 4.5 cm) or have one taken there (some offices offer this)
4. Fill out the form and submit it
5. Wait for the pickup notification (same 3-8 week timeline)
6. Return to pick up your card and set your passwords

### Method 3: Apply by Mail

1. Fill out the application form that came with your notification card
2. Attach your photo
3. Mail it using the prepaid envelope that was included
4. Wait for the pickup notification

Note: The prepaid envelope has an expiration date. If it has expired, ask your city office for a new application form and envelope.

## Required Documents

Here is a checklist of what you need:

**For Application:**
- Notification card (tsuuchi card) or Application ID
- Photo meeting the requirements above
- Residence card (zairyu card) for reference

**For Pickup:**
- Pickup notification postcard (sent to your address)
- Residence card (zairyu card)
- Notification card (tsuuchi card), if you still have it

If someone else picks up for you (limited cases only), additional documents are required. Check with your municipality for details.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How does the My Number Card relate to my Residence Card (Zairyu Card)?

They are separate documents with different purposes:
- Your Residence Card proves your immigration status and permission to stay in Japan
- Your My Number Card is for administrative and tax purposes

You need both. The My Number Card does not replace your Residence Card. You are still legally required to carry your Residence Card at all times.

As of 2026, the Japanese government has been working on plans to eventually integrate Residence Card functions into the My Number Card system. Check the latest requirements with your local immigration office or city hall, as this integration is being phased in gradually.

### What happens when my visa / period of stay is renewed?

When you renew your visa or change your status of residence, you need to update the information on your My Number Card. Visit your local city or ward office after completing your immigration procedures. The digital certificate on your card will be updated.

Your My Number (the 12-digit number) stays the same. It does not change when you renew your visa.

### What happens when I move to a different address?

When you move within Japan:
1. Submit a moving-out notice (tenshutsu todoke) at your old city/ward office
2. Submit a moving-in notice (tennyuu todoke) at your new city/ward office within 14 days
3. Bring your My Number Card to the new office so they can update your address on the card

The digital certificates on your card will be temporarily suspended during the move and reactivated at your new address.

### I lost my notification card. Can I still apply?

Yes. Go to your city or ward office and request a new application form. They can look up your My Number and issue new application materials.

### Can I use my My Number Card at airports?

There are gates at some Japanese airports that allow My Number Card holders to pass through more quickly for domestic identity verification purposes. This is separate from immigration/passport control.

### What if I leave Japan permanently?

If you move out of Japan, you submit a moving-out notice at your city office. Your My Number Card becomes invalid. If you return to Japan in the future and re-register as a resident, you will need to apply for a new card. Your My Number (the 12-digit number) will be the same as before.

### Is there a fee?

The initial My Number Card application is free. If you lose the card and need a replacement, there is a small fee (typically around 1,000 yen for the card and 200 yen for the electronic certificate, though amounts may vary by municipality).

### How long is the card valid?

- For foreign residents: the card is valid until your current period of stay expires (the expiration date on your residence card)
- When you renew your visa, you also update your My Number Card
- The digital certificates have their own expiration cycle and may need to be renewed separately

Check the latest requirements with your local city office, as policies on validity periods are subject to change.

### Can I use my My Number Card for banking?

Yes. Banks and financial institutions may ask for your My Number when you open an account or for certain financial transactions. Having the card makes this process smoother since it serves as both your ID and your My Number verification in one step.

### What about privacy? Is it safe to carry?

The IC chip on the card does not store your tax records, medical history, or bank account information. It only contains the basic information printed on the card (name, address, date of birth, My Number) and digital certificates for authentication. The card also has a privacy protection feature: the My Number on the card can be hidden using opaque material on some versions, and the IC chip has security measures against unauthorized reading.

That said, treat your My Number Card like any important ID. Do not share photos of it online and keep it secure.

## Conclusion

Getting a My Number Card is one of those tasks that is easy to put off but increasingly hard to live without in Japan. It takes about 10 minutes to apply online and a single trip to city hall to pick up. In return, you get a valid photo ID, access to online government services, easier tax filing, and the ability to use your health insurance seamlessly at hospitals.

If you have been in Japan for a while and still have not applied, now is a good time. The process is straightforward, it is free, and it will save you time and hassle in the long run.
