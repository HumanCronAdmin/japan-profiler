So you just landed in Japan, you’re standing at a convenience store, and the cashier is staring at you while you fumble through a pile of coins you don’t understand. Been there. The good news? Japan has gone through a massive cashless payment revolution over the past few years, and in 2026, you can get through most of daily life without touching a single yen coin – if you know which apps to set up.

Here’s the thing: Japan used to be one of the most cash-dependent developed nations on earth. Even in 2020, cash accounted for roughly 70% of all transactions. Fast forward to 2026, and the government’s push toward a cashless society has completely changed the landscape. QR code payments are everywhere. Transit cards work at most stores. Contactless credit cards are finally mainstream.

But as a foreigner, it’s not always obvious which services actually work for you, which ones require a Japanese bank account, and which ones are just going to waste your time. This guide breaks it all down.

The Big Picture: How Cashless Payments Work in Japan#

Before diving into specific apps, here’s what you need to know about the cashless ecosystem in Japan:

  • QR code payments (PayPay, LINE Pay, etc.) – You scan a store’s QR code or they scan yours. Dominant at small shops, restaurants, and izakayas.
  • IC cards / transit cards (Suica, PASMO, ICOCA) – Tap-to-pay cards originally designed for trains but now accepted at convenience stores, vending machines, and many shops.
  • Contactless credit/debit cards – Visa/Mastercard contactless (tap-to-pay) acceptance has expanded significantly.
  • Mobile wallets (Apple Pay, Google Pay) – Link your cards and tap your phone. Works with both transit cards and credit cards.
  • Japan-specific e-money (Rakuten Edy, nanaco, WAON) – Mostly tied to specific store chains. Less useful for foreigners.

The key takeaway: no single payment method covers everything. You’ll want at least two or three options set up.

Cashless Payment Comparison: What Works for Foreigners in 2026#

Here’s a breakdown of the major cashless payment options and how foreigner-friendly each one is.

PayPay#

  • Type: QR code payment
  • Foreigner-friendly: Yes, with caveats
  • Where it works: Practically everywhere – convenience stores, supermarkets, restaurants, taxis, small local shops, even some shrines
  • Coverage: The single most widely accepted cashless payment in Japan. Over 60 million users.
  • Setup for foreigners:
    • Download the PayPay app (iOS/Android)
    • Register with a Japanese phone number (you need at least a prepaid SIM or eSIM with a Japanese number)
    • Verify your identity (passport + residence card for full features, or limited mode for tourists)
  • How to charge:
    • Link a Japanese bank account (if you have one)
    • Charge at convenience store ATMs (7-Eleven, Lawson, FamilyMart) using cash – this is the easiest method for newcomers
    • Some international credit cards can be linked, but support varies – verify current compatibility
  • Pros: Accepted almost everywhere, frequent promotional campaigns with point-back deals
  • Cons: Requires a Japanese phone number, full features need identity verification
  • Best for: Daily shopping, restaurants, splitting bills with Japanese friends

Suica#

  • Type: IC transit card / e-money
  • Foreigner-friendly: Very
  • Where it works: All trains/buses in major metro areas, convenience stores, vending machines, many restaurants and shops
  • Coverage: Accepted at most places displaying the IC card logo (interoperable with PASMO, ICOCA, and other regional IC cards)
  • Setup for foreigners:
    • Physical card: Available at JR station ticket machines (deposit of 500 yen, refundable). Note: physical Suica cards have had limited availability in recent years due to chip shortages – check current stock at stations
    • Mobile Suica (iPhone): Add directly through the Wallet app on iPhone 8 or later. Works even without a Japanese credit card – you can add money via Apple Pay with an international Visa/Mastercard
    • Mobile Suica (Android): Available through Google Pay on phones with FeliCa chips. Most Android phones sold outside Japan do NOT have FeliCa. Google Pixel 7 and later models sold globally do support it – verify your device
  • How to charge:
    • At station ticket machines (cash)
    • Through the mobile app (linked credit card)
    • At convenience store registers (cash – just ask “Suica ni charge shitai desu”)
  • Pros: Super fast tap-and-go, no need to unlock your phone, works offline on trains
  • Cons: Maximum balance of 20,000 yen, not accepted at as many small shops as PayPay
  • Best for: Transportation, convenience stores, vending machines, quick purchases

PASMO#

  • Type: IC transit card / e-money
  • Foreigner-friendly: Very (same as Suica)
  • Where it works: Same places as Suica – they’re fully interchangeable at payment terminals
  • Key difference from Suica: Issued by private railway companies in Tokyo (Suica is JR East). Functionally identical for payments.
  • Setup: Same process as Suica. Mobile PASMO also available on iPhone.
  • Should you get Suica or PASMO? Either one works. Most foreigners default to Suica because it has slightly broader name recognition and Mobile Suica was available first. If you commute on a private railway line (Tokyu, Keio, Odakyu, etc.), PASMO commuter passes might be more convenient.
  • Best for: Same use cases as Suica

Credit Cards (Visa / Mastercard / Amex)#

  • Type: Contactless + chip-and-pin
  • Foreigner-friendly: Yes
  • Where it works: Major chains, department stores, electronics stores, hotels, many restaurants. Acceptance at small independent shops is hit-or-miss.
  • Setup: Bring your existing international credit card. No additional setup needed for chip transactions. For contactless tap-to-pay, add your card to Apple Pay or Google Pay.
  • Important notes:
    • Visa and Mastercard have the best acceptance in Japan
    • Amex works at many places but not as widely
    • JCB is a Japanese brand – great acceptance domestically, less so abroad
    • Discover/Diners Club have limited acceptance
    • Some older terminals still only accept chip-and-pin, not contactless
    • Always carry at least one physical card as backup
  • Foreign transaction fees: Check with your card issuer. Cards with no foreign transaction fees will save you 1-3% on every purchase
  • Best for: Larger purchases, hotels, department stores, online shopping

Apple Pay / Google Pay#

  • Type: Mobile wallet
  • Foreigner-friendly: Yes
  • Where it works: Anywhere that accepts contactless credit cards or Suica/PASMO
  • Setup:
    • Apple Pay: Add your credit/debit card through the Wallet app. You can also add a Suica or PASMO card directly.
    • Google Pay: Add your card through the Google Wallet app. Mobile Suica support requires a FeliCa-enabled device (see Suica section above).
  • Pro tip: With Apple Pay, you can set Suica as your “Express Transit” card, which lets you tap through train gates without unlocking your phone or authenticating. This is a game-changer during rush hour.
  • Best for: Combining transit and shopping payments in one device

LINE Pay#

  • Type: QR code payment
  • Foreigner-friendly: Partially
  • Where it works: Convenience stores, many chain restaurants, drugstores
  • Current status: LINE Pay has been scaling back its standalone payment service. Many of its functions are being integrated into other services. Its acceptance footprint is smaller than PayPay.
  • Setup: Download LINE (the messaging app), then activate LINE Pay within it. Requires a Japanese phone number.
  • How to charge: Convenience store cash charge, linked bank account, or LINE Pay card
  • Should you bother? Honestly, if you already have PayPay, LINE Pay is redundant for most foreigners. The main reason to have LINE is for messaging (everyone in Japan uses it), and LINE Pay is just a bonus feature inside the app.
  • Best for: People who are already heavy LINE users

Rakuten Pay#

  • Type: QR code payment
  • Foreigner-friendly: Limited
  • Where it works: Many chain stores, convenience stores, drugstores
  • Setup: Requires a Rakuten account and a linked credit card. The app is available internationally but works best with a Rakuten credit card (which requires Japanese residency to apply for).
  • Best for: People who are deep in the Rakuten ecosystem (Rakuten Mobile, Rakuten Card, Rakuten Ichiba shopping)

Other Options You Might See#

  • nanaco: Primarily for 7-Eleven and Ito-Yokado stores. Not worth setting up unless you shop there constantly.
  • WAON: Primarily for AEON group stores. Same situation.
  • Rakuten Edy: Declining in relevance. Skip it.
  • d Barai: NTT Docomo’s QR payment. Only useful if you have a Docomo phone plan.
  • au PAY: KDDI’s QR payment. Only useful if you have an au phone plan.
  • Merpay: Tied to the Mercari marketplace app. Niche use case.

The 3 Apps Every Foreigner Should Set Up First#

If you’re just arriving in Japan and feeling overwhelmed, here’s your priority list:

1. Mobile Suica (via Apple Pay or Google Pay)#

Set this up on the plane. Seriously. You can create a Mobile Suica in Apple Wallet before you even land. Add 2,000-3,000 yen to start. The moment you step off the plane, you can tap through the train gates at Narita or Haneda and ride directly into the city. No fumbling at ticket machines, no lines, no confusion.

You’ll use Suica multiple times every single day – trains, buses, convenience stores, vending machines, coin lockers. It’s the backbone of getting around Japan.

2. PayPay#

Set this up on your first day. Once you have a Japanese phone number (even a prepaid SIM works), download PayPay and register. Load it up with cash at a convenience store ATM. PayPay fills the gaps that Suica and credit cards don’t cover – that tiny ramen shop, the local izakaya, the street food vendor at a festival, the barbershop in your neighborhood.

3. Your Existing Credit/Debit Card via Apple Pay or Google Pay#

Already done if you set up Suica. Make sure at least one international credit or debit card is added to your mobile wallet. This covers department stores, electronics shops, hotels, and online purchases. Having it on your phone means you can tap-to-pay at contactless terminals without digging out your wallet.

Bonus: If you plan to stay long-term, eventually get a Japanese credit card (Rakuten Card is one of the easier ones to apply for as a foreign resident). This opens up more options and earns you points in the Japanese rewards ecosystem.

Common Problems and How to Fix Them#

“My credit card got declined”#

  • Reason 1: Your home bank flagged the transaction as suspicious. Call your bank before your trip and let them know you’ll be in Japan.
  • Reason 2: The terminal only accepts certain brands. Ask “Visa wa tsukaemasuka?” (Can I use Visa?)
  • Reason 3: Some older terminals don’t support contactless. Try inserting the chip instead.

“I can’t register for PayPay without a Japanese phone number”#

You need a Japanese phone number. Options:

  • Buy a prepaid SIM at the airport (IIJmio, Mobal, and others offer data + voice SIMs)
  • Get an eSIM that includes a Japanese phone number (not all eSIMs do – data-only eSIMs won’t work)
  • If you’re a resident, sign up for a regular phone plan (Rakuten Mobile, ahamo, LINEMO, etc.)

“Mobile Suica isn’t working at the gate”#

  • Make sure your phone has enough battery. If your iPhone dies, Express Transit mode has a small power reserve, but don’t rely on it.
  • Check that Suica is set as your Express Transit card in Settings > Wallet & Apple Pay.
  • Make sure you’re tapping the top of your phone (where the NFC antenna is) flat against the reader.
  • If you get an error, check your Suica balance. Minimum fare balance is needed to enter the gate.

“The store says cash only”#

Yes, this still happens. Some traditional restaurants, small family-run shops, and certain institutions are cash-only even in 2026. Always carry 5,000-10,000 yen in cash as backup. You can withdraw cash from ATMs at 7-Eleven (7 Bank) and Japan Post – these are the most reliable ATMs for international cards.

“I got charged in yen but the amount looks wrong on my statement”#

Your card issuer converts the yen charge to your home currency using their exchange rate, plus any foreign transaction fee (usually 1-3%). The exchange rate used might differ from what you see on Google. This is normal. To minimize fees, use a card with no foreign transaction fees.

“Which convenience store ATMs work with foreign cards?”#

  • 7-Eleven (7 Bank ATM): Most reliable for international cards. English interface available.
  • Lawson (Lawson Bank ATM): Also supports international cards.
  • FamilyMart: E-net ATMs generally support international cards.
  • Japan Post ATM: Reliable for international withdrawals. Found in post offices everywhere.

Pro Tips from People Who’ve Been There#

  • Train station kiosks (NewDays, Kiosk): Always accept Suica. Fastest way to grab a drink or snack.
  • Vending machines: Most now accept Suica/IC cards. Look for the IC card reader (usually on the right side). Tap, select your drink, done in 3 seconds.
  • Tax-free shopping: When buying tax-free at electronics stores or department stores (purchases over 5,000 yen by tourists), you usually need to pay with a credit card or cash. PayPay and Suica purchases may not qualify for tax-free – ask the store.
  • Splitting bills: PayPay has a bill-splitting feature. If your Japanese friends use PayPay, you can send money directly to their accounts.
  • Budgeting: Load a fixed amount onto Suica or PayPay each week. When it runs out, you know you’ve hit your spending limit. Much easier than tracking individual credit card charges.
  • Receipts: Say “reshiito onegaishimasu” if you need a receipt. Cashless transactions still generate receipts at most stores.
  • Points and rewards: PayPay gives you points on purchases that can be used like cash. Suica doesn’t have a standalone points system, but JRE POINT can be linked to your Suica for rewards at JR East stations.

Final Thoughts#

Japan’s cashless payment landscape can feel overwhelming at first glance, but here’s the simple version: Suica for transit and quick taps, PayPay for everything else, credit card for big purchases, and a bit of cash for the holdouts. That four-part combo will get you through 99% of daily life.

Set up Mobile Suica before you even arrive. Download PayPay on day one. Keep your credit card in your mobile wallet. Carry a few thousand yen in cash. You’re good.

The best part? Once you’re set up, paying for things in Japan becomes incredibly smooth. A quick tap here, a QR scan there, and you’re on your way. No more panicking at the register trying to count out exact change in coins you can barely read.

Welcome to cashless Japan. Your wallet (and your coin purse) will thank you.
