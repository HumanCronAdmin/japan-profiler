You just landed in Japan. You have an apartment lined up, a job offer in hand, and exactly zero ways to receive your salary. Welcome to one of the most frustrating rites of passage for every foreigner in Japan: opening a bank account.

Japan runs on cash and domestic bank transfers. Your landlord wants rent via bank transfer. Your employer pays into a Japanese bank account. Your phone plan, utilities, and gym membership all auto-debit from one. Without a Japanese bank account, you are essentially locked out of daily life.

But here is the problem: Japanese banks were not designed with foreigners in mind. Language barriers, strict residency requirements, and a general wariness toward short-term residents mean that many newcomers get turned away at the door, sometimes multiple times, before they finally get an account.

This guide breaks down exactly which banks to approach, what documents to bring, and how to avoid the most common rejection traps. Whether you arrived yesterday or have been here for years and are still using a workaround, this is the guide you need.

Note: Banking policies change frequently. Always verify current requirements directly with the bank before visiting.

Why Is It So Hard for Foreigners?#

Before diving into the how, it helps to understand the why. Japanese banks operate under strict anti-money laundering (AML) regulations, and the Financial Services Agency (FSA) has tightened rules over the past several years. Banks are required to verify identity, residency status, and in many cases, the expected length of stay in Japan.

This creates a catch-22 for newcomers: you need a bank account to function in Japan, but banks want proof that you plan to stay long enough to justify opening one. If your residence card shows less than six months remaining, many banks will refuse you outright.

Additionally, most bank branches operate entirely in Japanese. Even when a bank officially offers English services, the branch staff may not speak English, and the forms are almost always in Japanese. This is not a complaint; it is simply something you need to prepare for.

Major Banks Compared: Which One Should You Choose?#

Here is a practical breakdown of the major options available to foreigners in Japan in 2026.

Japan Post Bank (Yucho Ginko)#

Japan Post Bank is often the first recommendation for newcomers, and for good reason. It has the lowest barrier to entry among traditional banks.

  • Foreigner-friendliness: High. Japan Post Bank is generally the most welcoming to foreigners, including those who have been in Japan for less than six months.
  • Required documents: Residence card (zairyu card), a form of secondary ID (health insurance card, My Number card, or passport with visa), and your seal (inkan) or signature.
  • English support: Limited. Some larger branches in Tokyo and Osaka have English-speaking staff, but do not count on it. The Yucho Directo online banking platform has partial English support.
  • Online banking: Available via Yucho Directo. The app and web interface have been gradually adding English functionality, though some sections remain Japanese-only.
  • ATM network: Massive. Japan Post ATMs are everywhere, including rural areas. They also accept many international cards.
  • Best for: Newcomers who just arrived, people in rural areas, anyone who wants the easiest approval process.

Tip: Go to a larger post office (not the tiny neighborhood ones) for the best chance of finding staff who can help with foreign applications.

MUFG Bank (Mitsubishi UFJ Financial Group)#

MUFG is Japan’s largest bank by assets and has the most extensive branch and ATM network among the mega-banks.

  • Foreigner-friendliness: Moderate. MUFG generally requires at least six months of residency, though exceptions exist for corporate-sponsored accounts.
  • Required documents: Residence card, passport, proof of address (utility bill or residence certificate from city hall), My Number notification, and an inkan or signature.
  • English support: MUFG has made significant efforts in English support. Their Global Business Banking services and select branches in major cities offer English-speaking staff. The online banking portal has an English option.
  • Online banking: MUFG Direct is available in English. The smartphone app supports English as well.
  • ATM network: Very large, with partnerships across convenience store ATMs.
  • Best for: People with stable employment at larger companies, anyone who needs a bank with strong international wire transfer capabilities.

Mizuho Bank#

Mizuho is another mega-bank with wide coverage, though it has fewer branches than MUFG.

  • Foreigner-friendliness: Moderate to low. Mizuho tends to be stricter with residency requirements. Expect to need six months or more of residency.
  • Required documents: Residence card, passport, proof of address, and an inkan (Mizuho branches are more likely to insist on an inkan rather than a signature).
  • English support: Limited. A few branches in central Tokyo and Osaka have English-capable staff, but this is not widespread. Online banking has minimal English support.
  • Online banking: Mizuho Direct is functional but primarily in Japanese.
  • ATM network: Good coverage in urban areas, less so in rural regions.
  • Best for: People whose employer specifically uses Mizuho for payroll (this is a common reason to open an account here).

SMBC (Sumitomo Mitsui Banking Corporation)#

SMBC rounds out the trio of Japanese mega-banks.

  • Foreigner-friendliness: Moderate. Similar to MUFG and Mizuho, the six-month residency guideline applies in most cases.
  • Required documents: Residence card, passport, proof of address, and inkan or signature.
  • English support: SMBC has been improving its English services. The SMBC Direct online banking has some English support, and select branches offer English assistance.
  • Online banking: Available with partial English support.
  • ATM network: Strong coverage, especially through convenience store ATM partnerships.
  • Best for: People whose employer banks with SMBC, or those in areas where SMBC branches are more convenient than alternatives.

Rakuten Bank#

Rakuten Bank is an online-only bank and a popular choice for foreigners who already have some residency history.

  • Foreigner-friendliness: Moderate. Rakuten Bank accepts applications from foreigners, but the application process is entirely online and primarily in Japanese. You typically need a residence card with more than one year of remaining validity.
  • Required documents: Residence card (photo upload), My Number, and a Japanese phone number.
  • English support: The banking app and website have English options that have improved significantly. Customer support in English is available via email.
  • Online banking: This is Rakuten Bank’s strength. Everything is managed online or through the app. No branch visits needed after setup.
  • ATM network: Rakuten Bank partners with convenience store ATMs (Seven Bank, Lawson, E-net). Free ATM withdrawals are available depending on your account tier.
  • Best for: Tech-savvy residents who prefer managing everything digitally, people already in the Rakuten ecosystem (Rakuten Card, Rakuten Mobile, etc.).

Sony Bank#

Sony Bank is another online bank that has built a reputation for being foreigner-friendly.

  • Foreigner-friendliness: High. Sony Bank actively markets to international residents and offers one of the better English experiences among Japanese banks.
  • Required documents: Residence card, My Number, and Japanese phone number. Application is done online.
  • English support: Strong. The website, app, and customer support are available in English.
  • Online banking: Excellent. Sony Bank’s app is well-designed and fully functional in English. Multi-currency accounts are available, making it useful for people who deal with multiple currencies.
  • ATM network: Partners with convenience store ATMs. Free withdrawals available depending on account level.
  • Best for: Foreigners who want a smooth English-language banking experience, people who send or receive money internationally, digital nomads with longer-term visas.

Shinsei Bank (Now SBI Shinsei Bank)#

SBI Shinsei Bank has long been a favorite among the foreign community in Japan.

  • Foreigner-friendliness: High. Shinsei has historically been one of the most welcoming banks for foreign residents.
  • Required documents: Residence card, passport, and Japanese phone number. Account opening can be done online or at select branches.
  • English support: Good. English website and app are available. Some branches have English-speaking staff.
  • Online banking: Solid online platform with English support.
  • ATM network: Free ATM withdrawals at convenience stores depending on your account stage.
  • Best for: Foreigners who want an account at a traditional bank with good English support, people who value free ATM access.

Wise (Formerly TransferWise)#

Wise is not a Japanese bank, but it deserves mention because it solves a specific problem that many foreigners face.

  • What it offers: A multi-currency account with a Japanese yen balance. You get a local-style account number that can receive domestic transfers in some cases, though it is not a full Japanese bank account.
  • Foreigner-friendliness: Very high. Wise is designed for international users.
  • Limitations: Not all employers or services accept Wise account details for salary payments or auto-debits. It is not a replacement for a Japanese bank account, but it is an excellent complement.
  • Best for: International money transfers, holding multiple currencies, bridging the gap before you open a proper Japanese bank account.

Revolut#

Revolut launched in Japan and offers another digital banking option.

  • What it offers: A multi-currency account with a Japanese yen option. Similar to Wise in concept.
  • Foreigner-friendliness: High, as it is designed for an international user base.
  • Limitations: Like Wise, it may not be accepted for all domestic banking needs such as salary deposits and utility auto-debits. Verify current acceptance before relying on it.
  • Best for: People who already use Revolut globally and want to keep their financial ecosystem consistent.

Step-by-Step: Opening a Bank Account Right After Arrival#

Here is the most efficient path to getting a bank account as soon as possible after arriving in Japan.

Step 1: Get Your Residence Card#

You receive your residence card (zairyu card) at the airport when you land (for stays over 90 days) or at your local immigration office. This is your most important document. Without it, no bank will talk to you.

Step 2: Register Your Address at City Hall#

Within 14 days of moving into your residence, you must register your address at the local ward or city office (shiyakusho or kuyakusho). This is legally required and also gives you a proof of address. While there, ask for a juminhyo (residence certificate). You will need this for some banks.

Step 3: Get a Japanese Phone Number#

Almost every bank requires a Japanese phone number. Get a SIM card or phone plan as soon as possible. Budget options like Rakuten Mobile, Povo, LINEMO, or IIJmio work fine. The bank needs to be able to call or text you at this number.

Step 4: Consider Getting an Inkan (Personal Seal)#

While many banks now accept signatures from foreign customers, some branches still prefer or require an inkan. You can get a simple one made at a hanko shop for around 1,000 to 3,000 yen. It does not need to be elaborate. Having one ready removes a potential obstacle.

Step 5: Choose Your Bank and Visit#

Based on the comparison above, pick your target bank. For newcomers with less than six months of residency, Japan Post Bank is the safest bet.

What to bring:

  • Residence card
  • Passport
  • Proof of address (juminhyo or utility bill)
  • Japanese phone number
  • Inkan or be prepared to use your signature
  • My Number card or notification letter (if you have it)
  • Cash for an initial deposit (as little as 1 yen at Japan Post Bank, though 1,000 yen is a reasonable starting amount)

Step 6: Visit During Quiet Hours#

Go to the bank on a weekday, ideally between 9:00 and 11:00 AM. Avoid month-end and the beginning of the month, as these are the busiest periods. Less crowded branches mean more time and patience from the staff to help with your application.

Step 7: Be Patient and Polite#

The process takes 30 to 60 minutes on average. If your Japanese is limited, having key phrases written down or using a translation app can help. Some people bring a Japanese-speaking friend for support, which is completely fine and often appreciated by the bank staff.

Step 8: Receive Your Cash Card#

Your bankbook (tsucho) is usually issued on the spot. Your cash card (ATM card) arrives by mail within one to two weeks. Make sure your mailbox has your name on it in the format the bank recorded, or the delivery may fail.

Common Rejection Reasons and How to Avoid Them#

Understanding why banks say no helps you avoid triggering those reasons.

“Your period of stay is too short.” This is the most common rejection. If your residence card shows less than six months remaining, most mega-banks will decline. Solution: Try Japan Post Bank first, as they are more flexible. Alternatively, wait until you renew your visa and try again.

“You do not have proof of address.” If you have not registered at city hall yet, you cannot prove where you live. Solution: Always register your address before visiting a bank.

“We cannot verify your identity.” This usually means the bank cannot match your documents or there is a discrepancy in name spellings between your passport and residence card. Solution: Make sure the name you provide matches exactly what is on your residence card.

“You do not have a Japanese phone number.” Banks need a way to reach you and increasingly use SMS verification. Solution: Get a Japanese phone number before visiting the bank.

“You already have an account at this bank.” Some banks limit foreigners to one account. If you had a previous account that was closed, this can cause issues. Solution: Bring any documentation related to previous accounts and explain the situation.

“This branch does not handle foreign applications.” Smaller branches sometimes redirect foreigners to larger branches. Solution: Call ahead or check the bank’s website for branches that specifically handle foreign customer applications.

The Digital Banking Alternative#

If traditional banks are giving you trouble, digital options can bridge the gap.

Wise lets you set up a Japanese yen balance quickly. While it is not a full domestic bank account, you can use it to receive international transfers and convert currencies at competitive rates. Some users have successfully used Wise details for certain domestic transfers, but this is not guaranteed for all services.

Revolut offers a similar proposition in Japan. The app-based experience is smooth, and if you are already a Revolut user from another country, adding a Japanese yen account is straightforward.

PayPay Bank (formerly Japan Net Bank) is another online option that some foreigners have used successfully, though the application process is primarily in Japanese.

These digital options work best as complements to a traditional Japanese bank account rather than replacements. For salary deposits, rent payments, and utility auto-debits, most services still require a standard domestic bank account.

Final Thoughts#

Opening a bank account in Japan as a foreigner is not impossible. It just requires the right preparation and the right bank. Here is the quick summary:

  • Just arrived, need an account fast: Go to Japan Post Bank.
  • Want the best English experience online: Look at Sony Bank or SBI Shinsei Bank.
  • Need a mega-bank for employer requirements: MUFG, SMBC, or Mizuho, depending on which your company uses.
  • Want everything digital: Rakuten Bank once you have enough residency history.
  • Need to move money internationally right now: Set up Wise or Revolut while you work on getting a domestic account.

Get your residence card, register your address, get a phone number, and walk into a bank with confidence. The process might take an afternoon, but once it is done, daily life in Japan gets significantly easier.

Banking requirements and policies change. Always verify current requirements with the specific bank before applying. This guide reflects general practices as of early 2026.
